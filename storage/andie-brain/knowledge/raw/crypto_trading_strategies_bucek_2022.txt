ABSTRACT

This bachelor thesis describes the problems that a programmer of automatic trading systems in the environment of cryptocurrencies may encounter. These problems may be the design of a strategy that takes into account a specific market, a currency pair, and technical capabilities of specific technologies and market exchanges.

The thesis describes some strategies, including the publicly known models Martingale and Turtle strategy, on which certain problems associated with automatic trading can be described.

The result of these analyzes and the result of the work is a created program that allows traders to effectively backtest strategies and thus simplify the work of traders.

Keywords: trading strategy backtesting, backtesting program, martingale analyzes, turtle strategy analyzes

CHAPTER 1: INTRODUCTION

Cryptocurrency trading has become more and more popular in recent years. The vision of getting rich quickly attracts more and more people who want to use this tool. Unfortunately, due to poorly set strategies and insufficient education from both a fundamental and trader point of view, many people end up in negative numbers.

Emotions play a significant role in these failures. Failure to manage personal discipline when a person sees large negative or even large positive numbers in the account often leads to bad decisions. Therefore, many people are looking for a tool to filter out their emotions and prove to be profitable. Various robots and automated scripts that trade according to predefined rules can be used. But if programming such successful robots was easy, everyone would do it, and everyone would be successful.

CHAPTER 2: TERMINOLOGY

Price Chart: Shows historical price movements and it is a basic tool used to analyze strategies. There are several different types of views.

Time frame (TF): The graph is divided into equal length intervals, where each interval is somehow specifically represented on the graph. Common designations: 1W (1 week), 1D (1 day), 4H (4 hours), 1H (1 hour), 5m (5 minutes), 1m (1 minute).

Open price (OP): Value at the beginning of a specific interval.

Close price (CL): Value at the end of a specific interval.

High: Highest value during the interval.

Low: Lowest value during the interval.

Candlestick chart: Price chart where for each period we record the values OP, CL, HIGH, LOW. The area between OP and CL is rectangularly colored with 2 colors according to the type of candle. Decreasing candles (where OP is greater than CL) are often colored red. Ascending candles (where OP is smaller than CL) are often colored green.

Price action (PA): A method that analyzes the graph based on price movement.

2.1 TRADE ORDERS

Market orders: Orders that say "I want to buy or sell and I do not care what the price will be when it is my turn in the queue to settle." Executed immediately at the current market price.

Limit orders: Orders that create an offer to trade at a specific price. "I want to sell or buy at a specific price." Creates liquidity in the market.

Stop market (Stop-loss): An order with a trigger price. Customers who want to buy have their Stop price set HIGHER than the current price; when that price level is reached, it is queued as a market order. Customers who want to sell have their Stop price set LOWER than the current price. Used to cover losses or protect profits in the event of an unfavorable market situation.

Stop limit: An extended Stop market. In addition to the Stop price, a Stop limit price is set, which defines the lowest or highest price the customer is willing to accept when their market order is queued.

Trailing stop: An expanded Stop market extended by a percentage deviation from the highest or lowest peak. The stop level recalculates according to the deviation and the current price. If the price breaks the stop-market price, the customer is queued for market orders. Used when a strategy hypothesizes that individual peaks and corrections in the trend are in the range of x percent.

Leveraged position: SPOT accounts allow trading only physical assets held in the account. MARGIN accounts extend this by significantly increasing tradable volumes through borrowed money. When opening a leveraged position, the trader makes a small deposit (margin), and the rest is financed by the broker. If the loss is greater than the margin, the broker automatically closes the trade — this is called a Margin call.

2.2 FEES

Spread: The difference between the current sale and purchase price. High spreads can significantly affect fast strategies that operate at low timeframes.

Volume charges: A charge on the volume of traded currency common on cryptocurrency exchanges, typically around 0.01–0.1%. Some exchanges distinguish between Maker fee (for limit orders that create liquidity) and Taker fee (for market orders that withdraw liquidity).

CHAPTER 3: DESIGNING STRATEGIES AND BACKTESTING SOFTWARE

3.1 DESIGNING STRATEGIES

In order to choose the right technologies needed in trading, automated bots programming and backtesting, a draft strategy must be established. The proposal needs to specify:
- A specific market or markets (stocks, currency pairs, commodities, etc.)
- Fundamental approach (world reports, price movement analysis, indicators, etc.)
- Algorithmic description of the strategy (individual conditions for signal to enter, exit, or adjust position size)
- Specific technologies and exchanges for execution

3.1.1 Negatives of Algorithmization

The basic problem is the lack of experience and intelligence in pattern recognition, as the robot works on the basis of conditions and mathematical models. Shortcomings include:
- Inability to analyze sudden changes in the market
- Difficulty with gradual, longer-term fundamental changes in market behavior
- Limited pattern recognition without AI assistance

3.1.2 Positives of Algorithmization

Absence of emotions: Many traders fail because they cannot control their emotions — fear of losing money and greed lead to non-compliance with the trading plan, premature closing of trades, incorrectly set stop-losses, and excessive trading without sufficient input signals.

Speed of data analysis: Automation enables immediate decisions, which can be very important for strategy profitability.

Combination of human and robot strengths: Manually created indicators (trend lines) combined with constant automated monitoring.

3.2 BACKTESTING AND TRADING PROGRAMS

3.2.1 Metatrader: Allows chart analysis, order execution and strategy automation. Uses mql4 and mql5 languages (built on C++). Has a Python library (MetaTrader5) for account connection. Currently two versions: MT4 and MT5.

3.2.2 NinjaTrader: Allows chart analysis and trading script creation using NinjaScript (built on C#). Free version for basic analysis; paid versions allow broker connection.

3.2.3 TradingView: Largest online platform and social network for traders, used by 30M+ traders and investors worldwide. Uses Pine Script language (similar to Python/Kotlin). Free version has limitations on number of indicators and alerts.

3.2.4 3Commas: Web application allowing connection and trading on different accounts. Has robots based on specific strategy foundations (e.g. DCA strategy) with configurable parameters. Allows connection to Telegram and Signal.

3.2.5 TrendSpider: Allows strategy creation using a simple design environment. Advantage: use of automated trendlines and supports.

CHAPTER 4: ALGORITHMIC DESCRIPTION OF THE STRATEGY

General Trading Principles:

Past profits do not guarantee future profits: While a strategy can be very effective on historical data, we always move on the basis of probability that principles may change. Some principles may be more reliable (long-term economic growth) than others (short-term cryptocurrency predictions).

Correlation does not imply causality: The fact that two phenomena are similar does not mean there must be a causal relationship between them. Optimizing indicator constants WITHOUT fundamental knowledge of why changes are made only leads to curve-fitting on historical data.

Capital protection: If unable to wait for a cyclical market to return, it is necessary to determine when to accept losses. Stop-losses help minimize the effect of negative events. Start with small risk percentages per trade and increase gradually.

4.1 INDICATORS

Moving Average (MA): An indicator used to detect a time series trend. Types:
- SMA (Simple Moving Average): SMA = (A1 + A2 + ... + An) / n
- EMA (Exponential Moving Average): EMA = (Value_today * (smoothing / (1+n))) + EMA_last * (1 - smoothing / (1+n))
  EMA gives more weight to recent periods.

RSI (Relative Strength Index): Measures the internal strength of an asset. An oscillator with a range of 0-100.
Formula: RSI_t(n) = 100 - [100 / (1 + U(n)/D(n))]
Where:
- U(n) = average of net growth closing changes over the period
- D(n) = average of net decline closing changes over the period

4.1.3 MA RSI Strategy Description

Using indicators RSI(40), MA(12), and MA(50), plus max_OP and min_OP (highest and lowest OP of last 20 candles):
- If a trade is open, wait for the faster MA(12) to cross the slower MA(50) and then close the trade.
- LONG open: Wait for RSI less than 50 and the MAs to intersect. SL is set to min_OP of last 20 candles. Risk per trade: 1%.
- SHORT open: Wait for RSI less than 50 and the MAs to intersect. SL is set to min_OP of last 20 candles. Risk per trade: 1%.

4.2 MARTINGALE STRATEGY

This strategy originated in mathematical models to increase chances of winning in gambling. Works on the basis of doubling the bet after an unsuccessful attempt (e.g., bet 100, lose → bet 200, lose → bet 400, etc.).

Key rules:
- If no trade is open, enter a trade in the direction opposite to the previous closed trade (default: LONG). This is the original level OP.
- Take profit (TP1) is fixed at 1% from entry in the direction of trade. The sl-trigger level (opposite direction) is also fixed at 1% from entry.
- When sl-trigger level is reached, open a trade in the opposite direction with TP2 at 1% from the sl-trigger. Multiple levels: TP, OP1=sl-trigger2, sl-trigger=OP2, TP2.
- A series of trades is closed only at levels TP1 and TP2.
- The result of a martingale series leads to a 0.5% profit, with initial trade size = 0.5 × deposit.
- Each time OP1 or OP2 is hit while trades are open, add a new trade so that upon subsequent achievement of TP1 or TP2, profit is 0.5%.
- Formula: |(TP1-OP1)/OP1 × OP1_position - (TP1-OP2)/OP2 × OP2_position| = 0.5/100 × deposit

Risk: Total volumes and potential losses rise very quickly. If position addition stops for technical reasons, or if fees are added (0.1%), the exchange may prevent opening new trades and in the worst case a margin-call occurs.

Fundamental basis is critical for this strategy — exponential growth of volumes means every small change has significant effect.

4.3 CONSTANT VALUE OF AN ASSET AT SELL AND BUY LEVELS (DCA Strategy)

Based on Dollar-Cost Averaging (DCA). The value of BTC in USD that we hold is kept constant (denote c). With price level difference constant k = 10%:

When price rises: Sell enough BTC so the remaining BTC value equals c. For k=0.1, sell k/(1+k) = 1/11 of BTC owned.
When price falls: Buy enough BTC to bring total value back to c. For k=0.1, buy k = 1/10 of BTC owned.

Profit formula for one up-and-down wave: gain = c × k × k/(1+k) = c×k²/(1+k)

Risk: Requires USD held in reserve for buying. If a large decline occurs, there may not be enough USD to continue the strategy.

Conclusion: If cyclicality and continuous long-term growth are assumed, HODL with buy/sell at specific parameters (FEAR & GREED index, RSI, etc.) may be more profitable in the long run.

4.4 PRICE ACTION STRATEGY

Based on swing theory and candle patterns. Swing low = red candle followed by green. Swing high = green candle followed by red. The price between these candles can represent support and resistance levels.

Candle patterns used:
- Hammer: (min(OP, CL) - LOW) / (HIGH - LOW) >= 0.7
- Shooting Star: (HIGH - max(OP, CL)) / (HIGH - LOW) >= 0.7

Rules:
- Enter Long if last swing low contains a Hammer candle. SL at LOW of swing low. TP at same distance from OP as SL.
- Enter Short if swing high contains a Shooting Star candle. SL at HIGH of swing high. TP at same distance from OP as SL.
- Risk per trade: 1%.

4.5 TURTLE STRATEGY

Origin: 1983. Created by Richard Dennis and William Eckhardt — a bet to test if anyone could learn to trade using predefined rules. The "Turtles" earned $175 million in 5 years. Core idea: "Trend is your friend."

Volatility (N): N = 20-day EMA True Range (ATR).
True Range = Max(High - Low, High - previous_close, Previous_close - Low)
N = (19 × PreviousN + Truerange) / 20

Position Sizing: size = (OP × deposit × risk) / |OP - SL|
Maximum trade risk: 2%

System 1 — Entry:
- Long open: price exceeds 20-day HIGH
- Short open: price drops below 20-day LOW

System 2 — Entry:
- Long open: price exceeds 55-day HIGH  
- Short open: price drops below 55-day LOW

Adding to positions: Add 1 Unit if price moves N/2 in the direction of the original trade from the fill price of the previous order.

SL Location: Long SL = 2N below entry. Short SL = 2N above entry. When adding to a position, all open trade SLs move N/2 higher.

System 1 — Exit:
- Long: price drops below 10-day LOW
- Short: price rises above 10-day HIGH

System 2 — Exit:
- Long: price exceeds 55-day high
- Short: price drops below 55-day LOW

Note: Originally used in the commodity market. Fundamentals of those markets have changed, so the strategy cannot be used in its original form in those markets.

CHAPTER 5: BACKTESTING PROGRAM

Backtesting was performed on timeframes 1W, 1D, 4H, 1H for markets BTCUSD, ETHUSD, ETHBTC, BNBUSD over the period 2017-08-18 to 2021-09-23.

Strategy Analysis Metrics:

RR_gain (Risk/Reward Gain) = (end_price - begin_price) / (begin_price - SL_price)
gain = deposit × risk_per_trade × RR_gain

If SL is reached, RR_gain = -1.

Drawdown: Historically worst period in terms of account loss. The maximum drawdown is tracked.

5.2 MA RSI STRATEGY RESULTS

1W timeframe: Very few or no trades. Results: 0 to -1.24 RR across symbols.
1D timeframe: Low trade counts. Results: -3.5 to +16.54 RR.
4H timeframe: -14.72 to +39.97 RR; highly variable with large drawdowns (up to 142 RR drawdown).
1H timeframe: High trade counts (57-275 per symbol). Results: -11.14 to +18.93 RR.

Conclusion: Strategy core has NO potential to develop further. Poor performance even without fees.

5.3 PRICE ACTION STRATEGY RESULTS

1W timeframe: Modest results, 0 to +2 RR.
1D timeframe: Negative results, -8 to -19 RR with large drawdowns.
4H timeframe: -32 to -77 RR. Large drawdowns (39-77 RR).
1H timeframe: Mixed results, -169 to +10.23 RR, with very large drawdowns.

Conclusion: Similar to MA RSI — no further development potential.

5.4 TURTLE STRATEGY RESULTS

System 1:
- 1W: Very small trade count, limited relevance. 9.45 RR for BTCUSD.
- 1D: Strong results — 37.67 to 101.62 RR. Max drawdown: 2.65 to 6.51. Absolute profit: 44% to 138%.
- 4H: 133 to 149 RR. Absolute profit: 212% to 297%.
- 1H: 228 to 315 RR. Absolute profit: 671% to 1813% (before fees). After fees ~90%.

System 2:
- 1W: Unusable, few trades, all negative.
- 1D: 18.69 to 99.86 RR. Absolute profit: 20% to 138%.
- 4H: 109.95 to 151.13 RR. Absolute profit: 179% to 291%.
- 1H: 213.77 to 265.93 RR. Absolute profit: 596% to 1061%.

Key Insight: October 2020 gained +60 RR in System 1 (1D), which significantly impairs the continuous ability of the strategy to generate profit. It is important to view data in context of continuous performance, not just absolute numbers. After fees and market-entry inaccuracies, absolute profit drops dramatically (e.g. +671% → ~90%).

Conclusion: Both Turtle systems meet current requirements and metrics. System 2 has fewer trades (about 1/3 less) but comparable absolute numbers. Numbers favor System 2 when fees are considered.

5.5 EVALUATION OF BACKTESTING STRATEGIES

Using the backtesting program, unusable strategies (MA RSI, Price Action) were effectively filtered out, allowing focus on the profitable ones (Turtle). 

Shortcomings of the backtesting program compared to professional platforms:
- No display strategy (inserting trades into a chart)
- No account appreciation over time chart
- Limited order types (no trailing or combined Limit/Stop-market orders by default)

CHAPTER 6: CONCLUSION

The goal was to create a backtest program for trading strategies over which the user has more control when working with data than existing platforms provide.

Key takeaways:
1. Past profits do not guarantee future profits.
2. Correlation does not imply causality.
3. Capital protection through stop-losses is essential.
4. Emotions cause most trader failures; automation removes emotional bias.
5. Turtle strategy shows strong performance at 1H and 4H timeframes, but October 2020 data significantly skews absolute results.
6. MA RSI and Price Action strategies showed no development potential in backtesting.
7. Fees and realistic market entry inaccuracies dramatically reduce apparent backtesting profits.
8. System 2 Turtle is favored over System 1 when fees are taken into account.
